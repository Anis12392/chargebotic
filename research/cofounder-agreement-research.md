# Co-Founder Agreement Research: Silicon Valley Best Practices

**Context:** Chargebotic (hardware startup, SF, pre-seed) — Anis (CEO) + Beau (co-founder, role TBD)
**Date:** February 2026

---

## 1. Standard Terms and Clauses in a Silicon Valley Co-Founder Agreement

Every co-founder agreement should include these core sections:

### A. Equity Allocation
- Percentage ownership for each founder
- Share class (common stock, typically at nominal/par value)
- Total authorized shares and issued shares
- 83(b) election filing (MUST file within 30 days of stock purchase to avoid massive tax liability)

### B. Vesting Schedule
- Standard: 4-year vesting with 1-year cliff
- Monthly vesting after cliff (1/48th per month)
- Acceleration provisions (single-trigger vs double-trigger)

### C. IP Assignment
- ALL intellectual property created by founders is assigned to the company
- Covers pre-incorporation work as well
- Includes patents, trade secrets, designs, code, hardware designs
- Critical for hardware startups where physical designs and prototypes have significant IP value

### D. Roles and Responsibilities
- Title and functional areas for each founder
- Decision-making authority by domain
- Full-time commitment clause
- Non-compete and non-solicit provisions

### E. Confidentiality
- NDA provisions
- Trade secret protection
- Scope and duration of confidentiality obligations

### F. Departure/Termination Terms
- Good leaver vs bad leaver definitions
- Buyback rights for unvested shares
- Treatment of vested shares upon departure
- Non-compete period post-departure

### G. Decision-Making and Governance
- Board composition
- Matters requiring unanimous consent
- Day-to-day operational authority
- Deadlock resolution mechanisms

### H. Capital Contributions
- Initial contributions (cash, IP, sweat equity)
- Future funding obligations or expectations
- Expense reimbursement policies

### I. Dissolution Terms
- What happens if the company shuts down
- Asset distribution
- IP ownership post-dissolution

## 2. Vesting Schedules: The Standard and Variations

### The Standard: 4-Year Vesting, 1-Year Cliff

This is the Silicon Valley default, endorsed by YC and virtually every VC:

- **Year 1 (Cliff):** 0% vests. If founder leaves before 12 months, they get nothing
- **Months 13-48:** 1/48th of total shares vest each month
- **At 48 months:** 100% vested

**Why the cliff exists:** Protects against a co-founder joining, contributing little, and walking away with equity. The 1-year cliff ensures minimum commitment.

### Acceleration Provisions

**Single-Trigger Acceleration:**
- One event (usually acquisition/change of control) triggers immediate full vesting
- Rare, mostly restricted to co-founders
- Acquirers dislike this (reduces retention incentive)

**Double-Trigger Acceleration (RECOMMENDED):**
- Requires TWO events: (1) change of control AND (2) termination without cause or resignation for good reason within 12-24 months
- Market standard approach
- Protects founders while keeping acquirers happy

### Good Leaver vs Bad Leaver

**Good Leaver (keeps vested shares):**
- Death, disability, retirement
- Termination without cause
- Resignation for good reason

**Bad Leaver (may forfeit even vested shares):**
- Fraud, gross misconduct, criminal conduct
- Material breach of agreement
- Competing with the company

## 3. Key Areas That MUST Be Covered

### Non-Negotiable Items (ranked by importance):

1. **Equity Split** - Percentage, vesting, cliff
2. **IP Assignment** - Everything belongs to the company (especially critical for hardware/patent-heavy startups like Chargebotic)
3. **Vesting Schedule** - 4-year, 1-year cliff minimum
4. **Roles and Responsibilities** - Who does what, reporting lines
5. **Full-Time Commitment** - Both founders must be full-time
6. **Decision-Making Framework** - How deadlocks are resolved
7. **Departure Terms** - Good/bad leaver, buyback rights, non-compete
8. **Confidentiality and Non-Compete** - Protect trade secrets
9. **Capital Contributions** - Who puts in what (cash, IP, time)
10. **Dissolution Terms** - Wind-down procedures
11. **Dispute Resolution** - Mediation, arbitration, jurisdiction
12. **Anti-Dilution and Future Funding** - Preemptive rights, pro-rata

### Hardware-Specific Additions for Chargebotic:

- **Physical prototype ownership** - Who owns the hardware prototypes
- **Patent filing responsibilities** - Who files, who pays, company owns all
- **Manufacturing IP** - Designs, supplier relationships, BOMs
- **Lab/equipment ownership** - If founders use personal equipment
- **Provisional patent assignment** - The $65 provisional patent must be assigned to the company

## 4. Common Mistakes Founders Make

### Mistake 1: No Written Agreement
A handshake is not enough. Verbal agreements are unenforceable and lead to devastating disputes.

### Mistake 2: Splitting Equity Based on the Idea
Overvaluing "who had the idea" vs future contributions. YC says: "All the work is ahead of you." The idea is worth very little compared to 4+ years of execution.

### Mistake 3: Not Having Vesting
Without vesting, a founder who leaves after 3 months walks away with their full equity stake. This is the single most common and most damaging mistake.

### Mistake 4: Deciding Equity Too Quickly
Research on 3,700+ founders shows that teams who split equity quickly (to avoid awkward conversations) have significantly worse outcomes. Harvard found unhappiness with equity splits increases 2.5x as startups mature.

### Mistake 5: Not Filing the 83(b) Election
Must be filed within 30 DAYS of stock purchase. Missing this deadline can result in enormous tax liability as the company grows in value.

### Mistake 6: Ignoring Deadlock Resolution
With two equal founders, every disagreement can become a deadlock. Must have a mechanism: tiebreaker vote, mediation, buy-sell provision.

### Mistake 7: No IP Assignment
Without formal IP assignment, a departing founder can claim ownership of critical technology they developed. For a hardware startup like Chargebotic, this could be catastrophic.

### Mistake 8: Trying to Renegotiate Later
YC reports the #1 reason for co-founder breakups in recent batches was attempting to renegotiate a previously agreed equity split.

### Mistake 9: Not Discussing Personal Circumstances
Founders don't discuss risk tolerance, financial runway, other commitments, or life changes that could affect commitment.

### Mistake 10: Skipping Legal Counsel
Template agreements are a starting point, but especially for hardware startups with IP considerations, a startup lawyer review is worth the $2-5K investment.

## 5. What Top Lawyers and VCs Recommend

### Y Combinator Recommendations

- **Equal equity splits preferred:** "Equity should be split equally because all the work is ahead of you"
- **Minimum 10% for any co-founder:** YC defines a co-founder as having at least 10% equity
- **Standard 4-year vesting, 1-year cliff:** "Just use the industry standard"
- **Discuss equity early:** "You should discuss and reach agreement on equity in one of your first conversations"
- **Write it down immediately:** Even before legal paperwork, put the agreement in writing (email counts)
- **Incorporate as Delaware C-Corp:** Standard for VC-backed startups
- **Use Clerky or Stripe Atlas:** YC-recommended incorporation services

### VC/Investor Perspective

- **Mark Suster (Upfront Ventures):** Warns that 50/50 splits can be a red flag to investors, as it may indicate inability to negotiate
- **Noam Wasserman (Harvard):** Teams that have deep equity discussions upfront are far less likely to encounter disputes later
- **Wilson Sonsini (top startup law firm):** Recommends double-trigger acceleration as market standard

### Deadlock Resolution Recommendations

For two-person teams, recommended mechanisms include:
- **Mediation first:** Low-cost, preserves relationship
- **Baseball arbitration:** Each side submits final position, arbitrator picks one
- **Neutral third-party tiebreaker:** Appoint an advisor or board member as tiebreaker
- **Buy-sell provision (Texas Shootout):** Sealed bids, highest bidder buys out the other

## 6. Best Practices for a 2-Person Founding Team at Pre-Seed

### Equity Split Options

**Option A: 50/50 Split**
- Pros: Simple, signals mutual respect, increasingly common (45.9% of 2-founder teams in 2024)
- Cons: Deadlock risk, some investors see it as a yellow flag
- Mitigation: Add a 51/49 voting arrangement or neutral tiebreaker

**Option B: 51/49 or Similar Near-Equal Split**
- Pros: One person has tiebreaker authority, signals clear leadership
- Cons: The "lesser" founder may feel undervalued
- Best for: When one founder is clearly the CEO/leader

**Option C: Unequal Split (e.g., 60/40, 65/35)**
- Pros: Reflects different contributions, risk levels, or roles
- Cons: Can breed resentment if not well-justified
- Best for: When founders contribute meaningfully different things (e.g., one is full-time, one is part-time)

### Recommended Approach for Chargebotic (Anis + Beau)

1. **Have the hard conversation early.** Discuss everything: roles, commitment level, financial runway, risk tolerance, what happens if one person wants out
2. **Agree on equity split based on future contributions**, not past work
3. **Standard 4-year vesting, 1-year cliff for both founders** (no exceptions)
4. **Double-trigger acceleration** in case of acquisition
5. **Clear role definition:** CEO (Anis) handles business, fundraising, strategy. Beau's role needs to be defined (CTO? COO? Hardware lead?)
6. **IP assignment is critical:** All hardware designs, patents, and prototypes must be assigned to the company
7. **Deadlock resolution:** Consider a 3-person board (Anis, Beau, one independent/advisor) for tiebreaking
8. **Non-compete clause:** Both founders agree not to work on competing charging/robotics technology

### Incorporation Checklist

1. Incorporate as Delaware C-Corp (use Stripe Atlas at $500 or Clerky)
2. Issue founder shares at par value ($0.0001/share typical)
3. File 83(b) elections within 30 days (CRITICAL)
4. Sign IP assignment agreements (covering all pre-incorporation work)
5. Sign restricted stock purchase agreements with vesting terms
6. Set up a stock option pool (10-15% for future employees)
7. Get an EIN from the IRS
8. Open a business bank account

### Legal Resources and Templates

- **Clerky** (clerky.com): YC-recommended, handles incorporation + founder docs
- **Stripe Atlas** ($500): Incorporation + EIN + banking
- **Cooley GO** (cooleygo.com): Free legal document generators
- **NVCA Model Documents** (nvca.org): Industry-standard VC legal templates
- **Orrick Startup Toolkit**: Free legal docs for startups
- **fi.co/fast**: FAST agreement (for advisors, not co-founders)

### Recommended Budget

- Incorporation: $500-$1,000 (Stripe Atlas or Clerky)
- Co-founder agreement review by startup lawyer: $2,000-$5,000
- Provisional patent filing: $65 (already planned)
- 83(b) election filing: Free (just mail it to IRS)
- Total: ~$3,000-$6,500
